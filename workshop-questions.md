# Workshop Questions: Finding Your Next Customer

Techstars Chicago / Texas -- March 30, 2026

These questions help each team self-identify where they are in their GTM journey and what they need to do next to validate their niche. The goal is to go from a broad category ("restaurants") to a specific, winnable niche ("field sales teams that sell to restaurants and bars").

---

## The Niche-Down Ladder

Every company starts broad and needs to get specific. The questions below are designed to walk each team down this ladder:

1. **Who did you build this for?** (broad category)
2. **Who actually used it / paid for it / gave you the strongest signal?** (reality check)
3. **What made THAT person different from everyone else in the broad category?** (niche signal)
4. **Can you find 100 more of that exact person?** (scalability check)
5. **What would you Google to find them?** (operationalizability check)

If they can't answer #5, their niche isn't specific enough yet.

## LineSight

**Current positioning:** AI copilot for metal service centers

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. You have one customer, Elite Steel. What does Elite Steel do every day that makes LineSight essential? Is it the slitting specifically, or something else?

2. If I gave you a list of 72,000 metal fabricators in the US, how would you filter it down to the 500 that would respond to a cold email tomorrow? What makes those 500 different?

3. Are all slitting operations the same? Is there a specific tonnage, number of machines, or type of order (custom cut-to-order vs. standard runs) where your product creates 10x more value?

4. When Elite Steel was running without you, who was doing the planning? Was it the owner? A floor supervisor? An engineer? That person is your buyer. Can you name their job title?

5. If you walked into a steel service center and looked around, what would you see that tells you "this company needs LineSight" vs. "this company is fine without it"?

**What they should realize:** "Metal service centers" is still too broad. The niche might be "service centers processing 50K+ tons/year with 3+ slitting lines that still plan in Excel." That's maybe 2,000 companies, not 72,000.

## Seated with Love

**Current positioning:** White-label wedding planning platform for venues

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. Have you talked to any wedding venues yet? What did they say? If not, why not? There are 6,676 wedding venues in Google Maps alone.

2. When a venue coordinator hears your pitch, what's the first objection? Is it "we don't need this" or "we already have something" or "we can't afford this"? Each one tells you something different.

3. At $14.99/month, your product costs less than a single floral arrangement. Why isn't every venue signing up? What's the real blocker?

4. Is there a type of venue where the pain is 10x worse? A venue hosting 100 weddings/year has a very different problem than one hosting 10. Which one did you build for?

5. Have you thought about who at the venue actually opens their laptop and uses your product? Is it the owner, the coordinator, or an assistant? That person determines whether this sticks.

**What they should realize:** The pricing suggests they need massive volume to build a business. At $15/month, 1,000 venues = $15K MRR. They either need to go upmarket (multi-venue operators, hotel chains) or find a wedge where venues pay more (vendor marketplace with referral fees). The question isn't "can we find venues" but "which venues will pay enough to matter."

## Plexus

**Current positioning:** HardwareOps, observability for physical systems

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. Scout Space is your one customer. Walk me through the exact moment they decided they needed Plexus. What broke? What were they using before? How did they find you?

2. You serve "hardware teams." That's like saying Datadog serves "software teams." Datadog started with DevOps teams at web-scale SaaS companies. What's your equivalent? Who is in so much pain that they'll rip out their current setup this month?

3. If I put you in a room with 10 autonomous vehicle engineers, 10 satellite operators, and 10 robotics founders, which group would lean forward the most when you describe Plexus? Have you tested this?

4. Your product starts at $500/month. That means your buyer has budget authority or can expense it. Is this an individual engineer buying it on a credit card, or does it need a procurement process? That changes everything about how you sell.

5. What's the search query or job posting keyword that identifies a company in your ICP? If someone posts a job for "Telemetry Engineer" or "Fleet Operations Lead," is that your signal?

**What they should realize:** They need to pick ONE vertical and go deep. "Hardware teams" is a category, not a niche. The exercise should be: pick autonomous vehicles, aerospace, OR robotics. Then find 10 companies in that vertical, find the hardware/ops engineer at each, and send 10 cold emails this week. The responses (or silence) will tell them more than any market analysis.

## WattShift

**Current positioning:** Energy management platform for channel partners

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. You have four partner segments on your website: solar installers, DER OEMs, HVAC contractors, property managers. Have you closed a deal in any of them? Which one is closest?

2. Solar installers in California are getting crushed by NEM 3.0. That's a clear pain trigger. But when you pitch a solar installer, do they immediately get it? Or do you have to explain the value for 20 minutes? The speed of that "aha" tells you if you've found the right niche.

3. You're B2B2C, which means you need your partner to sell your product to their customer. That's a double sale. Have you seen a partner actually pitch WattShift to a homeowner? What happened?

4. If I gave you a list of 6,000 solar installers in California, would you email all of them? Or is there a subset, maybe installers doing 50+ installs/year, who are big enough to care about software but small enough that they don't have an in-house energy team?

5. Your product is free to end users and you make money from grid compensation. Have you proven the unit economics? How much revenue does one connected thermostat generate per year? That number determines whether this is a venture-scale business or a lifestyle business.

**What they should realize:** Four segments is three too many for their stage. They should pick the one segment where the "aha" happens fastest. If it's solar installers in NEM 3.0 markets, the niche is: "residential solar installers in California doing 50-500 installs/year who lost their battery upsell." That's maybe 500 companies.

## Offline

**Current positioning:** IRL advertising network connecting brands to micro-communities

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. You have 1,500 communities in your network. How many of those have actually run a campaign with a brand? What percentage of your supply side is active?

2. When you pitch a brand, do they compare you to influencer marketing, experiential marketing, or something else entirely? The comparison they make tells you which budget you're competing for.

3. Your best case study (Supersuite) showed 10x brand awareness and 78% product trial. That's incredible. Why isn't every DTC beauty brand banging down your door? What's the real sales blocker?

4. On the host side, run clubs are interesting. But a run club with 50 members and a run club with 5,000 members are completely different businesses. Where's the sweet spot? Too small and brands don't care. Too big and they already have sponsors.

5. If you could only work with ONE type of community in ONE city for the next 6 months, what would it be? Run clubs in LA? Supper clubs in NYC? Dance communities in Chicago? That answer is your niche.

**What they should realize:** Two-sided marketplaces are the hardest businesses to build. They need to decide which side to lock down first. The run club example is powerful because it's specific enough to build a repeatable playbook: find the organizer, check their LinkedIn, verify they have a day job, reach out with "you're growing something special but you can't quit your job to do it. What if brands paid you?" If that message resonates with 8 out of 10 run club organizers, they've found their niche.

## CloudReno

**Current positioning:** AI-enabled kitchen & bath renovation roll-up

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. You're looking for 3 "founding partners" out of 24,000 renovation businesses. That's a 0.01% acceptance rate. What does a founding partner look like that the other 23,997 don't? Be brutally specific.

2. Have you talked to any renovation business owners yet? When you describe CloudReno, do their eyes light up or do they get defensive? The emotional reaction matters more than the words.

3. You say you can grow a business 50% in year one. How? Is that through better marketing, better estimating, more leads, higher close rates, or operational efficiency? The answer determines what kind of business you're actually building.

4. An owner who's 55 and thinking about retirement has very different motivations than a 45-year-old who's just burned out. Which one is actually your buyer? The retiree wants a payout and legacy preservation. The burned-out operator wants help scaling. Those are different pitches.

5. When a renovation business owner Googles "sell my remodeling business," what do they find? Who are you competing with for their attention? Business brokers? Private equity? Other roll-ups? Or are they not even searching yet?

**What they should realize:** Their biggest challenge isn't finding K&B remodelers. There are 42,000 in our dataset. The challenge is identifying which owners are psychologically ready to sell. That's not a data problem, it's a relationship and outreach problem. The niche might be: "kitchen and bath remodelers in the Southeast doing $2-5M revenue, owner is 55+, 4+ stars on Google with 100+ reviews, no website redesign in the past 5 years." That last signal (outdated website) might indicate an owner who stopped investing in growth.

## Kaizen Design

**Current positioning:** Digital building standards governance platform for health systems

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. How many health systems in the US have the problem you're solving? Not how many hospitals. How many health SYSTEMS with 10+ facilities and an active capital program? Can you name 10 right now?

2. You came from Stanford Health and Cleveland Clinic Abu Dhabi. When you were at those organizations, who would have bought this product? What was their title? What was their budget? Who else needed to approve?

3. Your product saves 5-15% on capital project budgets. For a $100M project, that's $5-15M in savings. That's a massive ROI story. Have you put that number in front of a health system CFO yet? What did they say?

4. Healthcare is one of the slowest-moving industries for software adoption. What's your realistic sales cycle? 3 months? 6 months? 18 months? That determines your runway math.

5. Is there a smaller version of this problem you could solve faster? Instead of digitizing an entire health system's building standards, could you solve one project's standards for one facility? A wedge that proves value in 30 days instead of 6 months?

**What they should realize:** Their addressable market might be 50-100 health systems. That's not a list-building exercise. That's an account-based sales motion where they need to know the name, title, email, and recent construction project of every VP of Facilities at every qualifying system. The exercise for them isn't "filter a database." It's "build a target account list of 25 health systems and find the decision-maker at each one."

## ForgeBee

**Current positioning:** Pollination-as-a-service via automated bee production

**Questions to ask the team:**

1. You're selling the Queen Monitoring Cage to bee researchers right now. How many have you sold? To whom? What did they pay? That's your current business. Everything else is a vision.

2. The jump from "selling research equipment to universities" to "delivering pollination units to almond farms" is enormous. What needs to be true for step two to work? Is the Bee Factory built? Has a DPU been field-tested? What's the timeline?

3. Almond growers in California's Central Valley are your long-term market. Have you talked to any? Do they know you exist? Do they believe automated bee production is possible, or do they think it's science fiction?

4. There are maybe 6,000 almond growers in California. But they don't all buy pollination the same way. Some contract directly with beekeepers. Some go through brokers. Some are part of cooperatives. Which channel would you enter through?

5. Your team has world-class science credentials. Gene Robinson literally won the Wolf Prize in Agriculture. But the person buying pollination services at a 2,000-acre almond farm doesn't care about prizes. They care about: will the bees show up healthy, on time, and at a fair price? How do you translate your science advantage into that language?

**What they should realize:** Their GTM is staged, and that's okay. Right now, their niche is "bee researchers at R1 universities with USDA grants studying colony health." There might be 30 of those labs. They should know every one by name. The almond grower market is 3-5 years away. The exercise for them is: list every university bee research lab in the US, find the PI, and make sure every one of them knows ForgeBee exists.

## How to Use These Questions in the Workshop

1. **Read the ICP profile for each company** (5 min)
2. **Ask the first 2-3 questions** to the team in front of everyone (10 min)
3. **Let the team discuss and self-identify** where they are on the niche-down ladder (5 min)
4. **Assign the right exercise** based on their readiness:
   - **If they can't name 5 customers who said yes:** Exercise is customer discovery, not list-building. Write 10 outreach messages to named humans and send them during the workshop.
   - **If they have signal but it's broad:** Exercise is niche-down. Take their broad ICP, add 2-3 qualifying filters, and see if the remaining list is findable and reachable.
   - **If they know their niche:** Exercise is list-build. Use Google Maps data, SERP searches, or LinkedIn to build a list of 50 leads that match the refined ICP. Verify 5 of them live during the session.
5. **End with the Who/Why/Where/When framework** for each team (5 min)

| Readiness Level | Signal | Exercise |
|-----------------|--------|----------|
| Pre-validation | Can't name 5 people with the problem | Customer discovery: find 10 humans, draft outreach, send it |
| Broad ICP | Know the category but not the niche | Niche-down: add qualifying filters until the outreach writes itself |
| Validated niche | Have signal from real customers | List-build: find 50-100 leads matching the refined ICP |
